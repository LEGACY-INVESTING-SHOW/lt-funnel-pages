## SECTION 4: The 5 Ways People Try to Figure Out Their Taxes (And Why Most Fall Short)

**Section Heading:**
> THE FIVE WAYS PEOPLE APPROACH TAX PLANNING — AND WHAT ACTUALLY WORKS

**Sub-head:**
> Most people cycle through four of these before they find the fifth one.

---

**Option 1 — Googling Strategies:**
- Takes 8–12 hours per session
- Returns hundreds of strategies with no filtering for your income, state, or entity
- Leaves you with a pile of ideas and no order of operations
- You still don't know which ones apply to you
- **Result: 12 hours gone. Same tax bill.**

---

**Option 2 — Asking Your CPA Vague Questions:**
- "Am I missing anything?" gets a generic answer
- CPAs are reactive by nature — they file what you give them
- They rarely audit your full structure unless you push them to
- You leave the meeting unsure if you covered everything important
- **Result: Peace of mind you didn't actually earn.**

---

**Option 3 — Generic Tax Checklists:**
- Good for awareness, not for decisions
- Doesn't know your income, your state, your entity, your situation
- Can't rank what matters most for your profile
- Usually written for the average earner, not the $150K–$500K earner
- **Result: A list of 40 things. No idea which 4 actually matter for you.**

---

**Option 4 — Asking AI (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, etc.):**

This is where most smart, resourceful people land now. They open ChatGPT, paste in their income, their state, their business setup — and ask what tax strategies they should be using. They get a long, well-written answer.

And then, almost word for word at the bottom: *"This is not financial or tax advice. Please consult a qualified CPA or tax professional."*

There are two reasons AI keeps doing this — and neither of them are going away.

**The first reason is legal.** Every major AI company — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google — has compliance teams whose job is to prevent the AI from giving you specific financial guidance. No matter how detailed your prompt is, no matter how much context you provide, the answer will always be hedged. The liability forces it. You're not getting a plan. You're getting a disclaimer with extra steps.

**The second reason is that you don't know what to ask.** This is the one people don't see coming. You can feed AI everything about your situation — but you don't know what questions to ask because you don't know all the strategies that exist. You don't know what combinations of entity structure, income type, and state law unlock which moves. You don't know which qualifying conditions to flag. So you ask broadly, and you get broad answers. Generic, high-level, and safe. AI can only work with what you give it — and if you don't know what to give it, it fills in the gaps with the most common-case assumptions. Which are probably not yours.

The result is an answer that sounds personalized but is actually built for the average person. Which is exactly what you didn't want.

- **Result: A well-written answer that ends with "consult a CPA." You're back where you started.**

---
